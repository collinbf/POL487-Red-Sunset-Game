СОВЕТСКИЙ СОЮЗ — 1985
Briefing for Party Cadres

March 1985. Mikhail Sergeyevich Gorbachev assumes the office of General Secretary of the Communist Party of the Soviet Union. He is 54 years old — the youngest member of the Politburo to take power since Stalin.

The USSR he inherits is not the USSR of 1945, nor of 1961. It is a superpower in stasis. Eleven time zones. Three hundred million citizens. The world's largest nuclear arsenal. And an economy that has not grown in a decade.

Oil prices are falling. The war in Afghanistan grinds on. Corruption runs from the factory floor to the Politburo. A generation of elderly General Secretaries — Brezhnev, Andropov, Chernenko — died in office within three years of one another. The party apparatus is exhausted. The people are cynical.

Gorbachev believes the system can be saved. He will speak of glasnost — openness. Of perestroika — restructuring. Of uskoreniye — acceleration. He believes these are repairs, not replacements.

He is wrong about what they will become. But he does not know that yet.

Neither do you.
